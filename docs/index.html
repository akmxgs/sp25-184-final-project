The Butterfly Effect: Simulating Iridescence in Butterfly Wings and Other Nanostructured Materials

Summary + Goal

In this project, we plan to adapt our existing ray tracing code in order to render and simulate the irridescence caused by light diffracting on complex nanostructures. We seek to explore the ways altering these nanostructures or the position of the light source influences the diffraction of light and how we perceive the color of an object's surface.

We want to simulate the iridescent coloration of butterfly wings, particularly those from the Morpho genus, which display angle-dependent color shifts due to structural interference from multilayer nanostructures. Inspired by Sun et al. (2006), we will implement a simplified model of multilayer thin-film interference into an existing PathTracer from CS284 Homework 3.

Problem Description

The structural coloration found in Morpho butterfly wings is a fascinating natural optical phenomenon. Unlike pigment-based colors that absorb and reflect specific wavelengths to create the colors we see, these wings create vibrant, angle-dependent blues through nanoscale structures that cause light interference. This presents unique rendering challenges that traditional computer graphics approaches struggle to capture accurately. Traditional rendering systems tend to use simplified RGB models that cannot accurately represent such complex spectral interactions present in the real-world.

To solve this problem, we aim to create a custom BSDF inspired by Sun et al. (2006) to mimic angle-dependent color shifts. A challenge lies in balancing the realistic accuracy of our renderings with computational efficiency. Spectral rendering would create more accurate images, but this requires more computational power than traditional RGB approaches. If we use full spectral rendering for each frame, we risk lag time in our system as we intend to make the interface interactive to show different colors at different viewing angles.

Questions We Plan To Answer

  • What are the fundamental differences in how spectral power distributions (SPDs) and RGB values represent light, and how do these differences impact rendering accuracy?
  • What unique optical properties do nanostructured materials exhibit? How can they be represented in the ray tracer and how do their distributions differ from other BSDFs?
  • What are the challenges in achieving real-time rendering of materials with spectral behavior using a ray tracer? How can we optimize the system for real-time interactivity?

What We Plan to Deliver

  • Implementation of a custom BSDF that approximates multilayer interference using the empirical model in Sun et al. (2006).
  • Spectral approximation via precomputed wavelength-to-RGB mapping.
  • Initial tests on simple objects, like a sphere.
  • Series of images showing iridescence changes with light direction and viewing angle.
  • Full rendering of a Morpho butterfly, including bump mapping to model wing texture.
  • Visualizations of realistic color shifts in Morpho wings under varied lighting/viewing angles.

What We Aspire to Deliver

  • Extension of the PathTracer to simulate spectral rendering directly using SPDs instead of RGB.
  • Direct modeling of the butterfly wing ridge geometry (beyond bump/normal mapping).
  • Rendering extensions to other interference-based systems (e.g. peacock feathers, CDs, beetle shells).
  • Interactive interface for modifying nanostructure parameters to observe iridescence changes.

How We Plan To Measure System Quality

  • Visual realism assessment by comparing renderings with real-world iridescent surfaces.
  • System performance benchmarking via render time comparisons across different scene/material complexities.

Schedule

  • Week 1: Literature review, architecture design, and development environment setup.
  • Week 2: Implement custom BSDF, develop wavelength-to-RGB mapping, and test on simple objects.
  • Week 3: Render iridescence images, implement bump mapping, and start full Morpho butterfly rendering.
  • Week 4: Finalize rendering, conduct performance benchmarking, optimize for interactivity, and prepare presentation materials.
